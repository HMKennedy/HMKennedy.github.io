Book Ferret

Book Ferret is an IOS application allowing users to scan or search for a book to discover the book’s reading level, Lexile score, interest level, and quiz program information quickly and easily. User’s have the option of creating individual profiles for children saving their reading level and reading history.

Problem

When children read books within their reading level, they are engaged and challenged rather than bored or frustrated. However, reading levels are not printed on books, so adults have to search the web or make an educated guess as to weather or not a book is in a child’s reading range. In addition, many schools require students to take computerized reading proficency tests. Parents and students need to know what books have available quizes and the quiz point values for the books.

Solution

Book Ferret is an IOS application which allows users to scan or search for a book to discover the book’s reading level, Lexile score, interest level, and quiz program information quickly and easily. User’s have the option of creating individual profiles for multiple children. Saved profiles can inculde a child's reading level and Lexile score as well as a reading history to help parents remember what books their child has read. Book Ferret also allerts users if a scanned book is in a child's Lexile range.

Key takeaways

  • Book information should be obtained by scanning a book's ISBN or searching for a book by title, author, or keyword.
  • The ability to share a book's information page or a user's reading history is crucial.
  • None of the existing applications allowed users to create individual user profiles.
  • Existing competition only provides information for one quiz program. A new application should provide quiz information for the two major quiz programs Accelerated Reader (AR) and Reading Counts (RC).

User Research

I conducted a user survey targeting three groups I identified as potential users for the app: Parents, Teachers, and Librarians. My goals of the survey were to determine who would use the app, what they would use it for, and what features they would find useful. I wanted to know if Lexile was important to users and how widespread the use of online quiz programs were. I also wanted to determine users’ current methods for obtaining information on Lexile levels and quiz programs.

Parents

Parents used several methods to determine if a book was in their child's reading range. Some flipped through the books and made and educated guess about the reading level. Others had their children read a couple pages to see if it was a good fit, and a few went online to find the Lexile of the book.

Teachers

To determine the Lexile score of a book, teachers said they either search for the book’s Lexile score online or they level it themselves using established educational guidelines.

Librarians

User Personas

Melissa | Informed Parent

Motivation

Melissa, a mother of three, takes Lexile range into consideration when selecting reading materials for her children. Her children's school participates in the Reading Counts quiz program, so it is important she select books that have quizes available.

Goals

  • Save reading histories for each child
  • Determine a book's Lexile quickly with a smartphone
  • Receive book recommendations

Frustrations

  • No mobile site for the Reading Counts program
  • Has trouble keeping track of her children's changing Lexile scores

Maria | Experienced Teacher

Motivation

Maria is a second grade teacher for a school district that uses the Accelorated Reader quiz program. She is often asked by parents and students the Lexile score of a book and if it has a quiz.

Goals

  • Obtain Lexile cores for classroom books
  • Quickly inform parents the Lexile and quiz information for a book
  • A mobile app would be ideal as she is rarely at her desk

Frustrations

  • Accelorated Reader website is cumbersome
  • Not enough time to level classroom books herself

Hailey | Well-Read Librarian

Motivation

Hailey is a childrens librarian at a branch that serves a variety of school systems which use differnet reading quiz programs. She is often asked by patrons the Lexile score of books and if they have reading quizzes available.

Goals

  • Quickly obtain a book's Lexile score and quiz information with one application
  • Provide patrons books recommendations tailored to their reading level
  • Maintain a reading history of the books she has read a story time

Frustrations

  • Currently has to visit multiple sites to obtain Lexile and quiz information
  • Must be at desktop computer to search for book information

User Stories

User stories were developed to determine what tasks different types of users would want to perform within the application. Some tasks included scanning a book’s barcode, creating a profile, and sharing a reading history. The various screens necessary to accomplish the tasks were listed and the user stories were organized into different user flows.

Top 5 High Priority User Stories

  • As a user, I want to scan a book's ISBN to find the book's Lexile score.
  • As a user, I want to search for a book by title.
  • As a new user, I want to create a user profile.
  • As a user, I want to save an item to my reading history.
  • As a user, I want to know if a book is in my quiz program.

Prototype and User Testing

I created a prototype in InVision using the low-fidelity wireframes. Wireframe testing allowed me to see if users could navigate the application with minimal visual clues. I conducted three in-person user tests asking testers to complete five tasks.

5 Tasks

  • Scan a book
  • Create a new user profile
  • Add a book to an existing user's reading history
  • Search for a book by title
  • Share an existing user's reading history via email

All three testers were able to complete all five tasks. When asked to share a reading history, two testers expected additional screens and where not sure they had completed the task. Based on my user testing I decided to add additional screens to add clarity.

Style Guide

I created a style guide to give the brand cohesion. The color palette includes navy, orange, yellow, blue, and turquoise which gives the app a vibrant and fun feel. For the brand typeface I wanted to invoke an old fashioned story book and chose Milonga. Because I was creating an IOS app, I wanted a typeface similar to San Francisco and decided on Roboto. I find Roboto clean and easy to read which was of utmost importance.

Iterations

Based on the user testing results and a design board review, I made several changes to the design mockups. I moved the Save button on the new profile screen to the top right so it is visible without needing to scroll. I moved the Delete button on the profile screen to the top right corner. On the introduction screens, I moved the Skip button to the top right corner, and I changed the bottom navigation bar to a light gray throughout the application.

What I Learned

As the mother of a seven year old, I knew an app of this nature would be helpful to me, but I was unsure if others would find it useful. I was pleased by the overwhelming enthusiasm participants in my user survey had for the app, and the insights I gained in the survey guided the project.

The most challenging aspect of the project was branding. I did not know what direction I wanted to take the branding, so I really struggled in the beginning. When I finally landed on the idea of Book Ferret, everything fell into place.

I relied heavily on user testing and comments from a design review board to iterate on my designs making them more intuitive. I realized from the beginning my designs would change and should change. If my final product looked exactly like my initial sketches, I did something wrong.

If given more time I would have added additional features to the app. I think book reviews and recommendations would be great additional features. An advanced search that allowed users to browse books by Lexile or interest level would be very helpful for adults who are looking for reading materials for children.
